why: >
  Quads work overtime absorbing load, especially downhill. And "IT band syndrome" is
  one of the most common runner injuries — but the band itself is tough fascia you
  can't really stretch. What you can release is the outer hip and glutes that tension
  it, and the whole side body. Saying that plainly earns real trust with runners.
angle: "You can't stretch your IT band — but you can release what pulls on it."
intro: >
  For the fronts of the thighs and the outer hip — the tissues behind most "IT band"
  complaints. Nothing forced; we're releasing what pulls on the knee, not yanking on it.
steps:
  - pose: "saddle"
    seconds: 120
    sides: 2
    note: "Half-saddle — one leg folded back, lean onto your hands or a bolster. The deep quad and hip-flexor opener, one side at a time. Skip if the knees complain."
  - pose: "twisted-dragon"
    seconds: 120
    sides: 2
    note: "From a low lunge, ease the front knee open with your hand — into the outer hip and the TFL."
  - pose: "banana"
    seconds: 120
    sides: 2
    note: "On your back, walk feet and shoulders to one side into a long crescent — the whole side line."
  - pose: "reclined-twist"
    seconds: 60
    sides: 2
    note: "Knees drop to one side — a gentle wring-out to finish."
scale:
  - level: "New"
    note: "Stay upright on your hands in the half-saddle rather than leaning back, and keep Twisted Dragon shallow."
  - level: "Returning"
    note: "Lean back onto your forearms or a bolster in the half-saddle, and open the outer hip a little more."
  - level: "Experienced"
    note: "Recline further in the saddle if the knees and back are happy, and hold the side-bend a full two minutes."
when:
