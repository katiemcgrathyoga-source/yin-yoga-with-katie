cues:
  - "Begin lying on your back, then let both knees drop over to your left."
  - "Slide the bottom (left) leg long down the mat, keeping the top (right) knee bent and resting toward the floor."
  - "Reach back with your right hand and catch the left foot or ankle — loop a strap around it if it's out of reach."
  - "Draw the foot gently toward you until you feel the front of that lower thigh open up."
  - "Open your left arm out to the side and turn your gaze softly that way."
  - "Find your edge — about 80% of your capacity, a real stretch but never forcing — then be still and let the shape do the work.   - "If the twist bites in the lower back, tuck a cushion under the top knee to take the strain."

faq:
  - q: "What does Cat Pulling Its Tail stretch?"
    a: "It's a reclining twist with an extra opening for the front of the body. As the spine and ribcage wring gently, catching the foot of the bent top leg draws a stretch through the quadriceps and hip flexor of that thigh, while the lower back and outer hip soften into the twist."
  - q: "How long do you hold Cat Pulling Its Tail in Yin Yoga?"
    a: "Held as a quiet reclining twist, 2–5 minutes per side works well. If you actively draw the foot toward you the pose becomes more active and yang-like, so keep those versions shorter — around a minute — or release the pull and simply rest in the twist."
  - q: "Is Cat Pulling Its Tail safe if you have lower-back or disc issues?"
    a: "Go gently. Keep the twist mild and let a cushion under the top knee take the strain rather than deepening the rotation. Many people won't be able to draw the foot in at all, and that's fine — the twist alone is the pose. Stop if anything feels sharp in the back or the knee."
  - q: "What's the difference between Cat Pulling Its Tail and a Reclined Twist?"
    a: "A Reclined Twist simply lets both knees drop to one side with the legs relaxed. Cat Pulling Its Tail keeps that twist but extends the bottom leg and catches its foot with the opposite hand, adding a quad and hip-flexor stretch and a mild backbend — a twist and a front-line opener in one shape."
