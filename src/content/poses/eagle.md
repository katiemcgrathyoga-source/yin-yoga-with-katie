benefits:
  - "Spreads and opens the upper back between the shoulder blades, a wonderful release for anyone who works at a desk."
  - "As the arms wrap and cross the body, the stretch settles into the backs of the shoulders — the posterior deltoids and the muscles around the shoulder blade."
  - "With the legs crossed too, it gently draws the outer hips into the opening."
  - "Encourages a slow, spacious breath into the back body, which feels calming and restful."
cues:
  - "Sit comfortably, cross-legged if you like. Reach both arms forward, then cross one arm over the other at the elbows."
  - "Bend the elbows and wind the forearms around each other, bringing the palms toward touching — or press the backs of the hands together if that is as far as they go."
  - "You can lift the elbows toward shoulder height to widen across the upper back — but if that brings a pinch or a bony 'stop' at the front of the shoulder, let the elbows settle a little lower. Lower arms often deepen the stretch across the back of the shoulders rather than jamming the joint."
  - "Find your edge — a real stretch across the shoulder blades but never forcing. Soften and let the shape do the work."
  - "To add the legs, cross one thigh over the other; for a simpler version, keep the legs in an easy cross and focus on the arms."
transitions_in:
  - "From a seated position, reach the arms forward and cross them at the elbows to begin the wrap."
  - "From Easy Pose, stay seated and take up the arm wrap."
transitions_out:
  - "Come out slowly. Unwind the arms and let them float down, then swap the cross of the arms for the second side."
  - "Roll the shoulders and reach the arms wide to release across the chest."
counterposes:
  - "Reach both arms wide or clasp the hands behind the back to open the chest after the wrap."
  - "Cow-Face arms to balance the shoulders with a different opening."
cautions:
  - "Whether your arms can fully wrap depends on your own bone structure, not on effort. A soft, bony 'stop' at the front of the shoulder is a hard limit — ease the elbows down rather than pushing through it."
  - "If wrapping the arms is too much for the shoulders or wrists, cross the arms and hold opposite shoulders in a gentle hug. Bending the arms is not cheating — it still delivers the stretch."
  - "Remember to change the crossing of the arms (and legs) on the second side so both shoulders share the work."
faq:
  - q: "How long do you hold Eagle in Yin Yoga?"
    a: "Most people hold Eagle for 1–3 minutes per side. The long, still hold is what makes it Yin — settle in, soften, and let the shape do the work. Come out a little sooner if you ever need to."
  - q: "What does Eagle stretch?"
    a: "Eagle arms mainly stretch the upper back between the shoulder blades and the backs of the shoulders, including the posterior deltoids. Adding the crossed legs brings a gentle opening to the outer hips as well."
  - q: "Why can't I wrap my arms in Eagle?"
    a: "Often it isn't tightness — it's your skeleton. The shape of your shoulder blade and collarbone decides how far the arms can cross before bone meets bone. If they won't wrap, that's normal: bend the arms, or simply hug opposite shoulders. You still get the stretch."
  - q: "What's the difference between Eagle arms and Cow-Face arms?"
    a: "Eagle wraps both arms in front of you across the chest, opening the upper back and backs of the shoulders. Cow-Face reaches one arm up and one behind the back to clasp, working each shoulder in opposite directions. They complement each other nicely in a shoulder-focused practice."
