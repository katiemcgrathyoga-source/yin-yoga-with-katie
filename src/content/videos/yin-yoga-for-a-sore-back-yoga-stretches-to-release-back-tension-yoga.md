## What this Yin class is

A slow, 40-minute Yin practice for a tired, sore back. We start lying down and stay unhurried the whole way through, holding each pose for around three to four minutes so the deeper layers of the back and spine have time to soften. We move gently — reclined twists to ease the spine, a hug-the-knees release for the lower back, a long forward fold for tight hamstrings, then a soft, supported back bend before a final side-lying twist. Think of it as a way to help ease the everyday tension a busy back carries, rather than fix anything — just a little care for a part of you that does a lot. Every pose has a lighter option and a deeper one, so you choose what feels right today.

## Who this class is for

Anyone whose back feels tired, tight, or a bit achy after a long day of sitting, standing, or doing more than usual. It's a lovely one to reach for in the evening, or any time your back is asking for some quiet attention. It's beginner-friendly too — no props to gather and nothing complicated to follow. One gentle note: this practice is meant for everyday soreness, so if you're living with a back condition, an injury, or pain that's sharp or ongoing, please check with a health professional before you begin.

## What you need

Just your mat and a warm, quiet space where you can be still and undisturbed. No props are required — though a cushion or folded blanket nearby is always welcome if you'd like a little padding under the knees or head. Wear something soft you can settle into, and feel free to pause the video any time you'd like to stay a little longer.

## How Yin Yoga works — three simple cues

- **Find your edge.** Come to about 80% of your capacity — a real stretch, but never forcing or straining, and always back off if anything feels sharp.
- **Hold for time.** Yin poses are held a few minutes; let the shape do the work and let each exhale carry a little tension out of your back. Come out slowly.
- **Be still.** Soften your jaw, your shoulders, your hips, and rest your attention on your breath — that quiet is where the release begins.
