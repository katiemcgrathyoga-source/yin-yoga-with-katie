Yin Yoga is a slow, quiet style of yoga where you settle into gentle, mostly floor-based shapes and hold each one for a few minutes, letting gravity — not muscular effort — do the work. It's one of the kindest ways to begin a practice: there's no flow to memorise, nothing to keep up with, and every pose has an easier option. Here's what it is, how it's different, and a simple way to start tonight.

## What is Yin Yoga, exactly?

Most of the yoga you'll see — vinyasa, power, flow — is what's often called *yang*: rhythmic, muscular, and quick. Yin is its opposite and its complement. Instead of moving through poses, you come into a shape, find a comfortable edge, soften your muscles completely, and simply stay — usually for two to five minutes.

That long, still hold is the whole point. In Yin, you're working the deeper, slower tissues of the body — the fascia, ligaments and joints — rather than the muscles, and those tissues respond to gentle, sustained time rather than effort. Grounded in the teaching of Paul Grilley and Bernie Clark, Yin is less about *getting into* a pose and more about letting the pose slowly work on you.

## How is Yin different from other yoga?

If you've tried a faster class and found it wasn't for you, this is the difference:

- **Yang styles** (vinyasa, power, most "flow") move continuously, build heat, and work the muscles with short holds. Wonderful for strength and energy.
- **Yin** is still and passive, with long holds, aimed at the connective tissue and the nervous system. It's quieting rather than energising.

You don't have to choose — many people do something active in the morning and Yin in the evening. But if energetic yoga has never quite fit, Yin is often the one that finally does.

## Do I need anything to start?

Very little. A mat or a bit of carpet, warm clothes you can be still in, and a quiet corner where you won't be disturbed. Props help you relax more deeply — a cushion or folded blanket to sit on, a bolster or a stack of pillows to rest over — but they're entirely optional. Come as you are.

## The three cues that make Yin work

Almost everything in Yin comes down to three simple ideas. Keep these in mind and you already know how to practise:

1. **Find your edge.** Come to about 80% of your capacity — a real, honest stretch, but never forcing or straining. Yin should feel like *a lot of something gentle*, not a little of something sharp.
2. **Hold for time.** Yin poses are held a couple of minutes or more; the length is where the change happens. Come out sooner whenever you need to.
3. **Be still.** Once you've found your shape, stop adjusting. Let your body grow heavy, rest your attention on your breath, and let the pose do the work.

## A few beginner-friendly poses

You could build a lovely first practice from just these — each links to its own page with cues, hold times and things to watch for:

- **[Child's Pose](/poses/childs-pose)** — a folded, grounding rest to begin and return to.
- **[Butterfly](/poses/butterfly)** — soles of the feet together, folding forward; opens the hips and the whole back line.
- **[Sphinx](/poses/sphinx)** — a soft, propped-up backbend for the lower back.
- **[Caterpillar](/poses/caterpillar)** — a long, calming forward fold down the back body.
- **[Reclined Twist](/poses/reclined-twist)** — a gentle wring-out for the spine, lying down.
- **[Corpse Pose](/poses/corpse)** — the final rest, where the practice quietly settles.

## How to start tonight

Two easy ways in, both free:

- **Press play on a beginner class.** [25-Minute Yin Yoga for Complete Beginners](/videos/25-minute-yin-yoga-for-complete-beginners) walks you through everything, gently, from start to finish — or try the [30-minute no-props beginner class](/videos/yin-yoga-for-beginners-30-minutes-no-props).
- **Follow a short routine with a timer.** If you'd rather practise without a screen narrating every second, a [self-guided routine](/routines) counts each hold for you and chimes softly when it's time to move on.

Practise in the hour before bed if you can — dim the lights, get warm, and let it be the slow end to your day.

*A gentle note: Yin isn't medical advice. Move slowly, take the easier option in any shape, and if you're pregnant or have a back, hip or knee concern, check with your doctor before you begin.*

The loveliest thing about Yin is that it asks so little and gives so much back. Start small — even fifteen or twenty minutes a few evenings a week — and let it become a place you return to.
