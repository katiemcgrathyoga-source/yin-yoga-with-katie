If your body is tired but your mind won't stop, you're not alone — and a short, slow Yin practice in the hour before bed can be one of the kindest ways to bridge the gap between a busy day and sleep. Yin Yoga asks almost nothing of you: you settle into a few floor-based shapes, hold each one for a few quiet minutes, and let the long, unhurried holds do the quieting. Here's how it works, the poses that help most, and a couple of short practices you can follow tonight.

## How does Yin Yoga help you sleep?

Sleep doesn't respond well to effort — the harder you chase it, the further it drifts. Yin works in the opposite direction: instead of *doing* something to fall asleep, you create the conditions that let sleep arrive on its own.

Two things make it so effective in the evening. First, the **long, still holds** — two to five minutes in each shape — give the body time to stop bracing. Second, the **slow, unhurried breathing** that comes with staying still is widely associated with a shift toward the body's rest state, the opposite of the wound-up, alert feeling that keeps you staring at the ceiling. Add dim light and a warm, quiet room, and you're gently signalling to your whole system that the day is over.

None of this is forceful. You're not stretching hard or working up a sweat — you're settling, softening, and letting gravity carry the weight of the day off your shoulders.

## When should you practise Yin for sleep?

The sweet spot is the **hour or so before bed**. Dim the lights, put your phone in another room (or at least face-down and silent), and get warm — a jumper and socks, a blanket nearby. You can practise right on your bed, or on the floor beside it so you can crawl straight under the covers afterwards. Loose, comfortable clothes, a cushion or two, and you're ready. Little and often does far more than long and rare, so even ten quiet minutes counts.

## The best Yin Yoga poses for sleep

You could build a lovely wind-down from just a handful of shapes. Each links to its own page with cues, hold times and things to watch for:

- **[Legs Up the Wall](/poses/legs-up-the-wall)** — the single most restful shape in all of Yin; a gentle inversion that quiets the whole system.
- **[Butterfly](/poses/butterfly)** — soles of the feet together, a soft fold forward to release the hips.
- **[Caterpillar](/poses/caterpillar)** — a long, calming forward fold down the back of the body.
- **[Banana Pose](/poses/banana)** — a quiet side stretch you can do while already lying down.
- **[Reclined Twist](/poses/reclined-twist)** — a gentle wring-out for the spine, on your back.
- **[Corpse Pose](/poses/corpse)** — the final rest, where the practice settles into sleep.

## How long before bed, and how often?

Practise **in the hour before you want to be asleep** — closer to bedtime is better, since the whole point is to carry the calm straight into sleep. As for how often: a few evenings a week is plenty to feel a difference, and nightly is lovely if it becomes a ritual you look forward to rather than another task. The consistency matters more than the length. If you only manage Legs Up the Wall and a few slow breaths, that's a complete practice.

*This is a gentle movement practice, not medical advice. If you have ongoing sleep difficulties, or you're pregnant or managing a back, hip or eye/pressure concern, take the gentler option and check with your doctor — especially before inversions like Legs Up the Wall.*
