If you run, you already know the feeling: you finish a good run with heavy legs, tight hips and that familiar pull down the back of your leg. You mean to stretch. Usually you don't. And it quietly stacks up — run after run — until a niggle turns into "maybe I'll take a week off."

That slow build-up of tightness is where most running injuries actually begin. And it's exactly what a short, slow Yin practice is made to undo. Here's how Yin helps runners, the poses that matter most, when to do them, and a few follow-along classes you can try tonight.

## Is yoga good for runners?

Running makes you fitter and stronger — and it also repeatedly loads the same chain of tissues: your hips, hamstrings, glutes, calves, quads and lower back. Over weeks, that repetition shortens and stiffens the very things that keep you running well. Recovery and mobility are the neglected half of training, and Yin fits that gap neatly.

Yin Yoga is slow and still. You settle into a shape and stay for a few quiet minutes, letting gravity and your breath do the work — nothing forced. Those long, unhurried holds are what reach the deeper tissue around the hips and joints that a quick 30-second stretch or a foam-roll only skims.

None of this replaces good coaching or physiotherapy, and it won't "cure" an injury — but for the everyday tightness running builds, it genuinely helps you move more freely and recover feeling less stiff.

## How does Yin Yoga help runners?

Two things do most of the work:

- **Range of motion.** Staying in a shape for two to five minutes gently improves how far your hips, hamstrings and ankles can move — mobility that running slowly erodes.
- **Recovery.** The long, quiet holds shift you toward your body's rest state. You come away feeling calmer and less braced — the recovery half of training that runners so often skip.

You don't need to become flexible. You just need to stop getting tighter.

## The best Yin Yoga poses for runners

Running doesn't tighten one thing — it tightens a chain, so it's worth working each link. Each pose below links to a full guide with cues and how long to hold it.

**For tight hips & hip flexors** — the runner's tightest link, made worse by sitting all day:
- [Dragon](/poses/dragon) — a deep low lunge, the big hip-flexor opener.
- [Sleeping Swan](/poses/sleeping-swan) — releases the glute and deep outer hip.

**For hamstrings & glutes** — the muscles that do the hard braking in every stride:
- [Caterpillar](/poses/caterpillar) — a long, calming fold down the whole back body.
- [Half-Butterfly](/poses/half-butterfly) — targets one hamstring at a time.

**For calves, ankles & feet** — the base most runners never work:
- [Toe Squat](/poses/toe-squat) — wakes up the soles and toes.
- [Squat](/poses/squat) and [Ankle Stretch](/poses/ankle-stretch) — free the ankles both ways.

**For quads & the outer hip** — the tissues behind most "IT band" complaints:
- [Saddle](/poses/saddle) — a deep opener for the fronts of the thighs (ease in gently, and skip it if your knees or back object).
- [Twisted Dragon](/poses/twisted-dragon) — into the outer hip and the muscles that tension the IT band. (You can't stretch the IT band itself — it's a tough band of fascia — but you *can* release what pulls on it.)

**For the lower back** — compressed by every run:
- [Sphinx](/poses/sphinx) and [Reclined Twist](/poses/reclined-twist) — gentle extension and rotation to undo the day's compression.

## When should runners do yoga?

Timing matters more than most runners realise:

- **After a run** is the sweet spot — you're warm, and the tightness releases before it settles in. Even ten quiet minutes counts.
- **On rest days**, a longer session resets the whole lower body.
- **Not deep stretching right before a run.** Long, passive holds are lovely for recovery, but done immediately before running they can leave you feeling loose and a little less powerful. Save the deep Yin for after and for rest days; keep anything pre-run short and gentle.

Little and often does far more than long and rare.
