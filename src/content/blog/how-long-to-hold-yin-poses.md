Most Yin Yoga poses are held for **two to five minutes**. If you're new, it's perfectly fine to start around **one to three minutes** and build up over the weeks; more experienced practitioners often settle in for five minutes or longer in the gentler shapes. But the exact number matters less than the principle behind it — so here's how to find the right hold for *you*, and how to know when it's time to come out.

## Why are Yin poses held so long?

In faster, "yang" styles of yoga, you might hold a shape for a few breaths — long enough to work the muscles. Yin is aiming somewhere deeper: the connective tissues, the fascia and the ligaments around the joints. Those tissues don't respond to quick effort the way muscle does; they respond to **gentle, sustained time**. A short stretch works the muscle and lets go; a long, still hold gives the deeper tissue the slow, patient load it responds to.

There's a second reason, and it might be the more important one: staying still for minutes at a time is deeply quieting. Long, unhurried holds and slow breathing are widely associated with a shift toward the body's rest state — which is exactly why Yin feels so calming, and why it's such a lovely practice for the evening.

## So how long is right for me?

Use these as a starting point, not a rule:

- **Brand new to Yin:** 1–3 minutes per pose. Let the shapes and the stillness become familiar first.
- **Comfortable with the practice:** 2–5 minutes is the everyday range.
- **Gentle, well-supported poses** (propped, reclined): you can happily stay 5 minutes or more.
- **Two-sided poses** (anything "per side"): give each side its own full hold.

The honest answer to "how long?" is: *long enough that the shape has time to work, and no longer than you can stay comfortably with your breath.* Some pose pages here suggest a range — see **[Caterpillar](/poses/caterpillar)**, **[Sleeping Swan](/poses/sleeping-swan)** or **[Saddle](/poses/saddle)** for a sense of typical hold times — but your body has the final say on any given day.

## Is holding *longer* always better?

No. Past a certain point, more time isn't more benefit — and pushing a long hold when you're straining or clenching can do the opposite of what Yin is for. The quality of your stillness matters more than the number on the clock. If you're gripping, fidgeting or bracing, that's your sign to ease off or come out, not to tough it out.

## How do I know when to come out?

Come out — gently and a little sooner — if you notice:

- Any **sharp, pinching or electric** sensation (that's a nerve or joint signal, not a stretch).
- **Numbness or tingling** in the limbs.
- That you're **holding your breath** or your mind has gone from calm to gritted.

When you do come out, do it slowly, and rest for a few breaths in a simple counterpose (a gentle **[Reclined Twist](/poses/reclined-twist)**, or hugging the knees in) before the next shape. After a long hold the joints can feel fragile for a moment — that passes.

## The easiest way to time your holds

You don't have to watch a clock. Every pose page on this site has a **built-in hold timer**, and if you'd rather practise a whole sequence without a screen narrating every second, a **[self-guided routine](/routines)** counts each hold for you and rings a soft bell when it's time to move on. You settle in, close your eyes, and let the timing take care of itself.

*This is a gentle movement practice, not medical advice. Ease off in any shape that doesn't feel right, and check with your doctor if you have a joint or back concern.*
