What this practice is

If you want to get flexible, this class is for you. Over about ninety minutes you'll gently stretch your shoulders, back, hips and legs — and by the end you'll be feeling lighter, looser and more flexible. It's all held the Yin way: long, slow, and unhurried, letting your breath and gravity do the work rather than forcing anything.

It's the most-practised class on the channel, and the reason people keep it running on the TV is simple — once you settle in, it carries you. And the more you come back to it, the more flexibility you'll gain.

Who it's for

Everybody, really. Whether you're brand new to Yin or well into your practice, every single pose has an option to stay gentle at the surface and an option to go that little bit deeper. So you get to choose where you're at today. If you sit a lot, feel stiff, or just want a long calming reset, come as you are.

What you'll need

Some sort of strap — a yoga strap, a belt, anything strap-like is fine. If you have blocks, keep them nearby; if you don't, don't worry too much about that, you'll be absolutely fine without them. And a warm, quiet space where you can be still for a while.

How Yin works — three things to remember

Find your edge. Come to the point where you can feel a real stretch — a slight challenge — but never pain, and never forcing. That edge is different in every body, and different every day.
Be still. Once you've found your edge, settle and commit to stillness. If your mind drifts away and starts to think, that's completely normal — just gently bring your focus back to your body, back to your breath.
Give it time. The holds here are long — around five minutes each. The length is where the change happens. If you need to come out of a pose sooner, please do; you can always work up to those five-minute holds.

A gentle reminder: Yin asks you to respect your limits, not override them. Come out of any pose that feels like pain rather than sensation, ease off if your hands tingle or a knee complains, and take your time coming out of the long holds — the body feels tender for a moment, and that passes. This practice is about taking good care of your body, never pushing it.
