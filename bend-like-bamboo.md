## What this practice is

This is a full-body flexibility class, held the Yin way: long, slow, and unhurried. Over about ninety minutes you'll work gently through the shoulders, upper back, hips, quads, hamstrings and spine — and by the end you'll feel lighter, looser, and more open. Nothing here is forced. Flexibility in Yin comes from staying, not straining, so the more often you return to this practice, the more it gives back.

It's become the most-practised class on the channel, and there's a reason people keep it running on the TV: once you settle in, it carries you.

## Who it's for

Anyone who wants to feel less tight and more free in the body — whether you're brand new to Yin or well into your practice. Every pose has an option to stay softer and an option to go deeper, so you decide the depth as you go. If you sit a lot, if you feel stiff, or if you simply want a long, calming reset, this one is for you.

## What you'll need

A strap (or anything strap-like — a belt, a scarf) is needed for a few of the poses. If you have blocks, keep them nearby; if you don't, you'll be fine without them. Most of all: a warm, quiet space where you can be still for a while.

## How Yin works — the three things to remember

This class gently teaches the three principles the whole practice rests on:

- **Find your edge.** Come to the point where you feel a real stretch — a slight challenge — but never pain and never force. That edge is different in every body and every day.
- **Be still.** Once you've found your edge, settle. Resist the urge to fidget, and when the mind wanders (it will — that's human), gently bring it back to the breath.
- **Give it time.** Yin holds are long — here, around five minutes per pose. The length is where the change happens. If you need to come out sooner, always do; you can build up to the full holds over time.

## What you'll practise

The class moves top to bottom, so the body opens in a natural order:

1. **Bow Tie** — a deep release for the shoulders and the sides of the upper back.
2. **Melting Heart (Anahatasana)** — opens the chest, shoulders and upper back.
3. **Butterfly** — the first hip opener, folding gently forward.
4. **Dragon** — a deeper opening through the hips, groin and the front of the back thigh.
5. **Shoelace with Cow-Face arms** — hips, spine and shoulders together; wonderful for posture.
6. **Saddle** — a long, mindful opening for the quads (with careful options for the knees).
7. **Dangling** — the one standing pose, lengthening the hamstrings and releasing the spine.
8. **Reclining hamstring stretch (with the strap)** — the hamstrings from a few angles.
9. **Reclined Twist** — restores the spine and brings the whole body back to balance.
10. **Savasana** — five minutes of rest, where the body absorbs everything you've done.

## A gentle reminder on your body

Yin asks you to respect your limits, not override them. Come out of any pose that feels like pain rather than sensation, ease off if the hands tingle or a knee complains, and take your time coming out of the long holds — the body feels tender for a moment, and that passes. This is about caring for your body, never pushing it.
